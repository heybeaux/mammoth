# Executive summary

Research question: What impacts do data centers have on the communities and environment around them?

Data centers can bring construction work, permanent technical jobs, tax revenue, and infrastructure investment, but their local costs can be large: concentrated electricity demand can delay fossil-plant retirements and require network upgrades; cooling can compete for water in drought-stressed basins; construction and standby generation add land, traffic, noise, and air-quality burdens; and weak siting processes can concentrate those burdens in already overburdened communities. The defensible answer is therefore conditional, not uniformly positive or negative.

The most immediate system-level pressure is electricity demand. Rapid demand growth has prompted PJM to review its definitions of firm service and its reliability obligations. Texas Senate Bill 6 indicates that regulators and policymakers may intervene in data center markets to address reliability and affordability concerns.

The sharpest local constraints are water availability and procedural fairness. Using recycled or non-potable water for data-centre cooling conserves potable water, especially in dry or drought-prone areas. Nearly half of approximately 700 reviewed data centers are located in census tracts with above-median environmental burdens.

Impacts are manageable only when project design and public rules make the trade-offs explicit. Policy changes such as Texas Senate Bill 6 indicate possible future intervention to address data-center reliability and affordability concerns.
